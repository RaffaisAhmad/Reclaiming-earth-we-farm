00 · Background

A Crisis
Built Over Decades

The Citarum River was once the lifeblood of West Java — clean, abundant, and sustaining 28 million lives. Today, the most dangerous threat it carries cannot be seen, smelled, or tasted.

Antimicrobial resistance (AMR) has been building silently for generations, shaped by the overuse of antibiotics in agriculture, hospitals, and factories. What began as a medical miracle is turning into one of the greatest emergencies of our time.

The WHO identifies AMR as one of the ten greatest threats to global health. By 2050, it could kill more people annually than cancer itself — unless the next generation of leaders acts.

01 · The Problem

A River Once Beautiful,
Now Invisible Danger

The Citarum River stretches approximately 300 kilometres through West Java. It is the biological and economic foundation of one of the most densely populated areas on Earth.

Antibiotic residues in water are conditioning bacteria to become untreatable. Continuously exposed to low concentrations of antibiotics from farms, hospitals, and factories upstream, bacteria develop resistance to the very medicines on which modern healthcare depends.

This case isn't isolated. From the Ganges to the Yellow River to the Mississippi — the world's waterways are being turned into incubators of drug-resistant bacteria. The WHO has identified antimicrobial resistance as one of the ten greatest threats to global health.

03 · A Human Story
Pak Ahmad, a rice farmer in a Citarum basin village, cuts his foot on a piece of rusty metal. The wound becomes infected. The doctor prescribes amoxicillin. Nothing happens. By the time he reaches a hospital in Bandung, the infection has spread to his bones. Antibiotic after antibiotic is tried. Nothing works. Pak Ahmad dies two weeks later from a wound that would have been resolved by penicillin in a previous generation. The bacteria responsible were resistant to every available treatment, shaped by the very river where he had farmed and bathed for decades.

Farm Antibiotic Use
Farmers administer antibiotics to poultry, pig, and fish feed to accelerate growth and prevent disease in crowded factory-farm conditions. Between 50–70% of each dose is excreted unchanged in animal waste, still biologically active. When spread as fertilizer on rice paddies, rainfall carries these active antibiotics directly into local streams that drain into the Citarum.
73% of all antibiotics: agriculture (Van Boeckel, 2019)
River Contamination
Antibiotic-laden runoff drains into waterways. Ciprofloxacin has been found at up to 50 µg/L in South Asian rivers — roughly 500× above the safe resistance-selection threshold. Tetracycline in West Java livestock wastewater reached 140–380 µg/L: concentrations sufficient to actively drive resistance, not merely indicate contamination.
500× above safe threshold · Larsson & Flach, 2022
Resistant Bacteria Form
Bacteria exposed to sub-lethal concentrations don't die — they adapt. Through gene mutations and horizontal gene transfer (HGT), they acquire resistance. In Citarum sediments: blaCTX-M (cephalosporin resistance), mcr-1 (last-resort colistin resistance), and tetracycline resistance genes tetA, tetB, tetM have all been detected at up to 10⁸ gene copies per gram.
mcr-1 gene detected · Bengtsson-Palme et al., 2018
Human Infection
Resistance genes travel in water droplets, on food surfaces, and through the hands of children into households. Routine activities — washing vegetables, eating river fish, swimming — create exposure pathways. Genetic pollution unlike plastic: it is irreversible on human timescales. Resistance genes persist in sediment for decades or centuries. Even if all antibiotic use ceased today, future bacteria could inherit resistance from ancestors that never encountered a single antibiotic molecule.
28 million people at risk · Citarum watershed
Treatment Failure — The Loop Closes
When resistant bacteria cause infection, standard antibiotics fail. Doctors resort to stronger last-resort drugs, which are more environmentally persistent — further selecting for resistance. This forces even stronger drugs in an escalating loop until no therapeutic options remain. AMR also degrades soil fertility, raises water treatment costs, accumulates in fish tissue, and kills the natural microbial communities that give rivers their self-cleaning capacity.
Prevention categorically superior to remediation

05 · Evidence-Based Solutions

Six Coordinated
Interventions

01
Enforce Antibiotic Growth Promoter Ban
Enforce Permentan No. 14/2017 through regular inspections, meaningful penalties, and transition subsidies for smallholders.
Policy
02
Mandate 60-Day Manure Composting
Composting for a minimum of 60 days before field application destroys 90–99% of resistant bacteria. Delivered through subsidised community facilities. (Manyi-Loh et al., 2018)
Agricultural Practice
03
Biochar Filters from Rice Husks
A 2022 study confirmed 94% removal of Pseudomonas aeruginosa and 88% removal of clarithromycin at ~$0.05/litre cost. (Xiang et al., 2022)
Technology
04
Farmer Training Programs
Training in hygiene, vaccination, and composting reduced antibiotic use by 40% in Thai poultry farms within two years — directly replicable in Indonesia. (FAO, 2019)
Education
05
Advanced Wastewater Treatment
Ozonation (99% bacterial removal), activated carbon filtration (90–95% antibiotic removal), and membrane bioreactors (99.9% removal) mandatory for all Citarum-basin factories. (Sundin et al., 2021)
Infrastructure
06
Bacteriophage Therapy & Probiotics
Phage therapy and probiotic supplementation reduced Salmonella infections in poultry by 70%, replacing antibiotics without generating resistance. (Callaway et al., 2018)
Biotechnology
06 · Framework

The One Health
Principle

The WHO, FAO, and the World Organisation for Animal Health (OIE) all endorse the One Health framework: the recognition that human health, animal health, and environmental health form a single, inseparable system.

Because AMR originates at the intersection of all three domains, no single-sector intervention is sufficient. The six solutions above are designed to address agricultural practice, environmental contamination, and human health simultaneously.

Protecting one domain requires protecting all three. This is not a medical problem. It is not an agricultural problem. It is a civilizational one.
