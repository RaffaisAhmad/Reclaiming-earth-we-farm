01 · The Problem

A River Once Beautiful,
Now Invisible Danger

The Citarum River was once the lifeblood of West Java — clean, abundant, and sustaining. Today, the most dangerous threat cannot be seen, smelled, or tasted.

Antibiotic residues in water are conditioning bacteria to become untreatable. Continuously exposed to low concentrations of antibiotics from farms, hospitals, and factories upstream, these bacteria develop resistance to the very medicines on which modern healthcare depends.

This case isn't isolated. From the Ganges to the Yellow River to the Mississippi — the world's waterways are being turned into incubators of drug-resistant bacteria. The WHO has identified antimicrobial resistance (AMR) as one of the ten greatest threats to global health.

02 · A Human Story
Pak Ahmad, a rice farmer in a Citarum basin village, cuts his foot on rusty metal. The wound becomes infected. Amoxicillin is prescribed. Nothing happens. By the time he reaches a hospital in Bandung, the infection has spread to his bones. Antibiotic after antibiotic is tried. Nothing works. He dies two weeks later from a wound that penicillin would have resolved in a previous generation.

03 · The Escalation Cycle

From Farm
To Human Infection

AMR drives a self-reinforcing escalation. Antibiotic emissions select for resistant bacteria → treatment failures → doctors use stronger last-resort drugs → more environmental persistence → resistance to those drugs → no therapeutic options remain.

Between 50–70% of each antibiotic dose given to livestock is excreted unchanged in animal waste. Spread as fertilizer on rice paddies, rainfall carries these active compounds into waterways — creating a continuous selection pressure on bacterial communities.

Most critically: unlike plastic pollution, genetic pollution is irreversible on human timescales. Resistance genes can persist in sediment for decades or centuries. Prevention is categorically superior to remediation.

04 · Evidence-Based Solutions

Six Coordinated
Interventions

01
Enforce Antibiotic Growth Promoter Ban
Enforce Permentan No. 14/2017 through regular inspections, meaningful penalties, and transition subsidies for smallholders.
Policy
02
Mandate 60-Day Manure Composting
Composting for a minimum of 60 days before field application destroys 90–99% of resistant bacteria. Delivered through subsidised community facilities.
Agricultural Practice
03
Biochar Filters from Rice Husks
Deploy at farm level. A 2022 study confirmed 94% removal of Pseudomonas aeruginosa and 88% removal of clarithromycin at ~$0.05/litre cost.
Technology
04
Farmer Training Programs
Training in hygiene, vaccination, and composting reduced antibiotic use by 40% in Thai poultry farms within two years — directly replicable in Indonesia.
Education
05
Advanced Wastewater Treatment
Ozonation (99% bacterial removal), activated carbon filtration (90–95% antibiotic removal), and membrane bioreactors (99.9% removal) mandatory for all Citarum-basin factories.
Infrastructure
06
Bacteriophage Therapy & Probiotics
Phage therapy and probiotic supplementation reduced Salmonella infections in poultry by 70%, replacing antibiotics without generating resistance.
Biotechnology
05 · Framework

The One Health Principle

Human health, animal health, and environmental health form a single, inseparable system. Endorsed by WHO, FAO, and OIE — because AMR originates at the intersection of all three domains, no single-sector intervention is sufficient.

06 · Call to Action

The Next Generation
Must Act Before the Window Closes

The knowledge exists. The solutions are proven. Indonesian students — living within the Citarum watershed — are not bystanders to this crisis. They are its most credible advocates, most adaptable innovators, and essential bridge-builders between evidence and action.

As advocates, as innovators, as bridge-builders — the leadership gap in AMR communication is precisely the space the next generation is best positioned to fill.
